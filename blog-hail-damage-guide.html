Introduction: Why Hail Damage Matters More Than You Think

Hail damage is one of the most underestimated roof problems. A spring or summer storm rolls through, your roof takes a beating, but from the ground everything looks fine. Six months later? You're dealing with leaks, missing shingles, and water damage inside your attic.

In El Paso, we see this scenario constantly. Hail storms are part of our climate reality, and your roof's ability to withstand them directly affects the lifespan of your entire home.

This guide covers everything you need to know about identifying hail damage, navigating insurance claims, deciding between repair and replacement, and preventing future damage.

How to Spot Hail Damage on Your Roof

Hail damage isn't always obvious from the ground. A small dent might not seem like much, but it's actually a weak point that invites water infiltration and accelerates deterioration.

Visual Signs of Hail Damage

Dented or Bruised Shingles
Look for circular or irregular indentations on asphalt shingles. Unlike wind damage (which tears or lifts shingles), hail creates small "dimples" that are sometimes subtle. Get up close—you're looking for shiny spots where the granules have been knocked off.

Missing Granules
Hail strips protective granules from shingles. If you see bare asphalt exposed or notice granules collecting in your gutters after a storm, that's hail damage.

Cracked or Split Shingles
Sometimes hail cracks shingles rather than just denting them. These splits are entry points for water.

Metal Flashing Dents
Check around chimneys, vents, and skylights. Metal flashing dents easily and loses its water-shedding effectiveness when dented.

Damage to Soft Materials
Hail can puncture or dent softer roofing materials like TPO (common on flat roofs) or gutters. Look for holes or soft spots.

The Granule Test

After a hailstorm, check your gutters and downspouts. If you find an unusual amount of granules (looks like coarse sand), that's a strong indicator your roof took impact. Take photos—this is valuable documentation for insurance.

Why You Can't Always See It From Below

Hail damage is often on the upper-facing surfaces of shingles, making it invisible from ground level. This is why professional inspections are essential.

Understanding Your Insurance Claim

Homeowner's insurance typically covers hail damage as part of your "perils" coverage. But the process is more nuanced than many people realize.

Does Your Policy Cover Hail?

Most standard homeowner's policies do cover hail. However:

  • Check your declarations page to confirm hail is listed as a covered peril
  • Review your deductible (often $500–$1,500, sometimes higher)
  • Note any exclusions for older roofs (some insurers won't cover roofs over 15–20 years old)

Steps to Filing a Hail Damage Claim

1. Document Everything
Take photos and video of damage immediately after the storm. Get wide shots and close-ups. This documentation is crucial—adjusters want evidence.

2. Call Your Insurance Company
Report the claim promptly. Provide the date of the storm, a description of damage, and any photos you've taken.

3. Schedule Your Adjuster Visit
The insurance company will send an adjuster to inspect your roof. They'll assess damage and estimate repair/replacement costs.

4. Hire a Roofer for a Second Opinion
This is critical. Get an independent assessment from a qualified roofer like Meraki. Insurance adjusters are trained to minimize payouts. A professional roofer can identify damage the adjuster might miss.

5. Review the Adjuster's Report
Compare the insurance estimate with your roofer's assessment. If they differ significantly, you can request the adjuster re-inspect or dispute their findings.

6. Supplemental Claims
If additional damage is discovered during repair (common with hail), you can file a supplemental claim for the extra work.

Common Insurance Claim Issues

Depreciation
Older roofs are subject to depreciation. Your insurance might pay replacement cost minus depreciation. Factor this into your budgeting.

Deductible
You'll pay this out-of-pocket. If your deductible is $1,000 and the damage is $5,000, insurance pays $4,000.

Denial or Under-Payment
Some adjusters are overly conservative. If you believe your claim was undervalued, a public adjuster or roofer advocacy can help.

Repair vs. Replacement: Making the Right Decision

Not every hail-damaged roof needs replacement. Sometimes repair is the smarter move. Sometimes it isn't.

When Repair Makes Sense

Single Impact Area
If hail damaged one section of your roof but the rest is in good condition, repair is often sufficient.

Young Roof (Under 10 Years)
If your roof is relatively new, localized repair buys you years of additional life.

Budget Constraints
Repair is cheaper upfront. If the roof is otherwise sound, repair can be a smart interim solution.

Aesthetic Reasons
If you're selling your home and want to patch visible damage, repair is quicker than replacement.

When Replacement is the Better Option

Widespread Damage (Over 20% of Roof)
Insurance and industry standards suggest replacement when damage exceeds 20% of the roof surface.

Older Roof (15+ Years)
If your roof is already near the end of its lifespan, replacement is more cost-effective than repair. Another hailstorm in a year or two will require replacement anyway.

Repeated Hail Damage
If this is your second or third hailstorm in 5 years, investing in an impact-resistant roof replacement prevents this cycle.

Water Intrusion or Rot
If hail damage allowed water into your attic, you may have hidden damage that requires structural assessment alongside roofing replacement.

Insurance Payout Covers It
If your insurance covers the full replacement cost, you're getting a new roof essentially for your deductible. Take the replacement.

The Hail-Resistant Roof Argument

If you're replacing due to hail, strongly consider impact-resistant shingles. The extra upfront cost is offset by:

  • Lower insurance premiums (10–20% discount)
  • Better performance in future hailstorms
  • Longer overall lifespan in an El Paso climate

Lesson Sometimes hail damage is an opportunity to upgrade.

Prevention: Making Your Roof More Hail-Resistant

You can't stop hail, but you can choose roofing materials better suited to El Paso's climate.

Impact-Resistant Shingles (UL 2218 Class 4)
These are your best defense. They cost 10–15% more but save on insurance and last significantly longer.

Metal Roofing
Exceptional hail resistance. Steel and aluminum roofs have few hail vulnerabilities.

Regular Maintenance
Keeping your roof clear of debris, maintaining seals, and catching small damage early prevents major problems.

Tree Trimming
Overhanging branches are hail's help—falling branches cause additional damage during storms.

Wrapping Up: Hail Damage Is Manageable

Hail damage is frustrating, but it's also manageable. The key is:

  1. Recognize damage early through regular inspections and post-storm checks
  2. Document thoroughly with photos and professional assessment
  3. Navigate insurance professionally with a roofer in your corner
  4. Make smart decisions about repair vs. replacement
  5. Invest in prevention through impact-resistant materials
