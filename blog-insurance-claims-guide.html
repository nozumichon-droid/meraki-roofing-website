Introduction: Stop Leaving Money on the Table

Most El Paso homeowners leave thousands of dollars on the table when filing roof insurance claims. The process is confusing by design. Insurance companies want to minimize their payout. You want to recover what you're entitled to. The gap between these two goals is where most families lose money.

This guide walks you through every step of the roof insurance claims process—from the moment you notice damage until you receive your final check. You'll learn exactly what insurance companies look for, what they often miss, and how to ensure you're not accepting an underpayment.

The difference between following this guide and winging it? Often $5,000–$20,000 on a single claim.

Step 1: Document Everything Before You Call Anyone

The moment you suspect roof damage, grab a camera or phone. Documentation is your power in this process. Insurance adjusters will use photos to justify their estimate. Your documentation—more thorough, more comprehensive—will counter that estimate.

What to Document Immediately

Date-Stamped Photos of All Damage
Take wide shots showing the full roof and close-ups of individual dents, cracks, or impact marks. Get photos of:

  • Shingle damage (every visible dent or crack)
  • Missing granules (the sand-like coating on shingles)
  • Gutters and downspouts
  • Metal flashing around chimneys, vents, and skylights
  • Siding and window frames
  • Any other potential storm damage

Video Walkthrough of Your Entire Roof
Walk around your home slowly, filming damage with narration. Say the date, describe what you're showing, and explain the damage. This video is powerful documentation that's harder for insurers to dispute.

Save Hail Reports and Weather Data
Screenshot or save:

  • National Weather Service hail reports for your area and date
  • Hail size (in inches) reported
  • Storm track maps
  • Any news coverage of the storm

Keep a Record of All Conversations
When you call your insurance company, note the date, time, name of the person you spoke with, and what was discussed. Send follow-up emails summarizing the conversation. This creates a paper trail that protects you if disputes arise.

Why Gutter Granules Matter
After a hailstorm, check your gutters and downspouts. You'll often find an unusual accumulation of granules—they look like coarse sand. Photograph these granules in place. They're visual proof of impact damage. Insurance adjusters specifically look for this evidence, and if you have it, it makes your claim much stronger.

Step 2: File Your Claim Promptly

In Texas, homeowners typically have up to one year from the date of loss to file a claim. But don't wait. The sooner you file, the better.

Texas Legal Requirements

  • Filing Window: Up to 1 year from date of loss (Texas Insurance Code § 2704.151)
  • Insurer Acknowledgment: Must acknowledge your claim within 15 days
  • Coverage Decision: Must make coverage decision within 15 business days after receiving documentation

What to Say (and Not Say) When You Call

DO:

  • State the specific date damage occurred
  • Describe the type of damage: "Hail damage to roof, gutters, and siding"
  • Mention you have documentation (photos, video)
  • Ask for the adjuster's name, claim number, and expected visit date

DON'T:

  • Admit fault or say "I'm not sure if it's damaged"
  • Estimate the repair cost yourself
  • Say you're "shopping for contractors" before the adjuster visits
  • Discuss hiring a public adjuster or attorney (wait until later if needed)
  • Agree to any estimate or settlement over the phone

Get Your Claim Number and Adjuster's Name
Write this down. You'll reference your claim number in every follow-up. You'll need the adjuster's name and contact info for scheduling.

Step 3: Get an Independent Roof Inspection

This is the critical step most homeowners skip—and it costs them thousands. Before the insurance adjuster visits, hire a professional roofer to inspect your roof. Here's why this matters.

Why You Need This Before the Adjuster

Insurance adjusters are trained to identify damage that justifies a payout while minimizing what insurance pays. A certified roofer is trained to identify all damage, including hidden damage and future problems. When your roofer meets with the adjuster, you have professional representation and a second set of eyes.

What a Certified Inspector Looks For

  • Direct impact damage (dents, bruises, cracks)
  • Compound damage (secondary impacts that weaken materials)
  • Soft metal damage (flashing, gutters, vents)
  • Underlying decking damage
  • Underlayment deterioration
  • Collateral damage (damage caused by the primary impact)
  • Potential hidden damage discoverable during tear-off

The Roofer's Scope vs. Adjuster's Scope
Your roofer's scope of work is what needs to be replaced or repaired to restore your roof to pre-loss condition. The insurance adjuster's estimate may be different—often lower. Your roofer's detailed scope becomes your defense if the adjuster underestimates work needed.

Step 4: Be Present During the Adjuster's Inspection

You have the right to be present during the adjuster's inspection. Exercise it. This is your home and your claim.

Your Rights During Inspection

  • Be present on the roof or supervise from below
  • Ask questions about damage identification
  • Have your roofer present (critical)
  • Request clarification on the adjuster's findings
  • Take your own photos and notes
  • Respectfully disagree with damage assessment

Bring Your Roofer to Meet the Adjuster
This is your best leverage. When your certified roofer is present, they can:

  • Point out damage the adjuster misses
  • Explain why certain damage is covered
  • Discuss potential hidden damage discoverable during tear-off
  • Challenge underestimations in real-time
  • Provide professional credibility to supplement claims later

Common Items Adjusters Miss or Under-Assess

  • Soft Metal Damage: Flashing, gutters, downspouts, vents. These dent easily and lose water-shedding function. Adjusters sometimes count them as "cosmetic"—they're not.
  • Collateral Damage: When a shingle is hit, it tears, splits, or dents other nearby shingles. This secondary damage is often missed.
  • Granule Loss: Adjusters may see granule loss but underestimate how many shingles are affected.
  • Flashing Integrity: Metal flashing doesn't need to be bent in half to fail—small dents allow water intrusion.
  • Underlayment Damage: Not visible without tear-off, but hail can puncture underlayment, creating future leak points.
  • Decking Damage: Hail can crack wooden roof decking. Adjusters often miss this until repair begins.

Don't Accept the First Estimate
The adjuster's initial estimate is often conservative. You're not obligated to accept it. You can request re-inspection, supplemental claims for hidden damage, or professional dispute resolution.

Step 5: Understanding Your Insurance Payout

Insurance payout structure confuses most homeowners. Understanding these terms is essential to knowing whether you're getting a fair settlement.

RCV vs. ACV: Which Do You Have?

Replacement Cost Value (RCV)
This is the full cost to replace your roof with new materials today. If your roof replacement costs $20,000, RCV is $20,000. This is better for you.

Actual Cash Value (ACV)
This is replacement cost minus depreciation. A $20,000 roof that's 10 years old might be valued at $12,000 under ACV because the insurance company assumes the roof had some remaining life.

Check your declarations page or policy document. Most homeowners in El Paso have RCV coverage, but some have ACV. This makes a massive difference in your payout.

How Depreciation Works

Insurance depreciates roofing materials at various rates depending on material and age. Asphalt shingles typically depreciate:

  • Years 1-5: Minimal depreciation (5-10%)
  • Years 5-10: Moderate depreciation (15-30%)
  • Years 10-15: Significant depreciation (30-50%)
  • Years 15+: Substantial depreciation (50-70% or more)

Recoverable vs. Non-Recoverable Depreciation
Under RCV policies, depreciation is often recoverable. This means insurance pays you replacement cost upfront, and you recover the depreciated amount when you submit proof of repair (invoices from your contractor).

Example: Your roof replacement costs $20,000. Insurance depreciates it by $5,000 (25%). They pay $15,000 upfront. You complete repairs and submit invoices proving $20,000 spent. You get paid the additional $5,000.

Your Deductible and How It Applies

Your deductible is your out-of-pocket responsibility. Common deductibles for roof damage are:

  • $500–$1,000 (standard)
  • 1% of home value (higher for valuable homes)
  • 5% of home value (increasing in high-hail areas)

The deductible applies to the total claim. If your roof damage is $8,000 and your deductible is $1,000, insurance pays $7,000.

Reading Your Insurance Estimate Line by Line

When the adjuster provides their estimate, review it for:

  • Labor rates: Do they match current El Paso contractor rates?
  • Material costs: Are shingles priced at current market rates?
  • Scope of work: Do they include all damaged materials?
  • Overhead and profit: Contractors typically mark up 15-30%; is this included?
  • Missing items: Did they include flashing, underlayment, decking assessment, disposal?

Ask your roofer to review the estimate and compare it to their assessment. Discrepancies justify supplement claims.

Step 6: The Supplement Process—Where the Real Money Is

Supplements are additional claims filed when damage is discovered during repair that wasn't visible in the initial inspection. Industry data shows supplements increase payouts by an average of 15-20% on hail claims. This is where additional money often comes from.

What Are Supplements and Why They Matter

When your roofer begins removing damaged shingles, they may discover:

  • Cracked or compromised roof decking
  • Deteriorated underlayment
  • Rotted wood or structural damage
  • Additional soft metal damage not visible from above
  • Collateral damage from primary impacts

These are all valid supplemental claim items. Your insurance is obligated to pay for repairs needed to restore your roof to pre-loss condition.

Hidden Damage Discovered During Tear-Off

Once shingles come off, the truth emerges. Decking may be cracked or warped. Underlayment may be punctured or deteriorated. Flashing may have rust or corrosion damage. All of this is supplementable.

This is why choosing the right contractor matters. Meraki documents every discovery, photographs it, and properly files supplements on your behalf. We don't hide damage to save insurance money—we expose it to ensure you get full coverage.

Common Supplementable Items

  • Roof decking replacement (full or partial)
  • Underlayment replacement or repair
  • Flashing repair or replacement
  • Code upgrades required during repair (ventilation, structural, etc.)
  • Additional soft metal damage (gutters, downspouts, vents)

Step 7: What to Do If Your Claim Is Denied or Underpaid

Claim denials and underpaymentsare more common than homeowners realize. Don't accept rejection without a fight.

Request a Re-Inspection

You have the right to request the adjuster re-inspect your roof. Provide new evidence or corrections to the original estimate. Meraki has successfully challenged initial denials by providing detailed documentation the first adjuster missed.

Get a Second Opinion from a Licensed Roofer

If your claim is denied or underestimated, hire a certified roofer (independent from the contractor who will do repairs) to provide a damage assessment. This assessment becomes your evidence if you dispute the claim.

Public Adjusters: What They Do and Cost

If disputes persist, you can hire a public adjuster. They:

  • Represent your interests against the insurance company
  • Investigate damage independently
  • Negotiate with the insurer
  • Prepare documentation for disputes or appraisal

Cost: Public adjusters typically charge 5-15% of the claim increase they obtain. So if they negotiate an additional $10,000 payout, they take $500–$1,500.

Texas Department of Insurance Complaint Process

If you believe your insurer acted in bad faith, you can file a complaint with the Texas Department of Insurance:

  • File online at tdi.texas.gov or by phone at 1-800-578-4677
  • Explain the issue clearly with documentation
  • TDI investigates and can penalize insurers for violations
  • This is free and independent of your claim

When an Insurance Attorney Makes Sense

If your claim is wrongfully denied, amounts are significant ($10,000+), or bad faith is evident, consult an insurance attorney. Many work on contingency (no upfront cost; they take a percentage of recovery). Texas law allows recovery of attorney fees in bad faith cases, so you may not bear the cost.

Common Insurance Mistakes That Cost El Paso Homeowners Thousands

1. Waiting Too Long to File

While Texas law allows up to 1 year, delays hurt your case. Fresh damage is easier to document. Memory is clearer. Insurance adjusters are more likely to approve fresh claims. File within days or weeks of damage discovery.

2. Not Documenting Damage Immediately

By the time you call a roofer, days have passed. Weather may have worsened damage. Original granule loss is gone from gutters. Photograph damage the day you discover it. This contemporaneous documentation is powerful evidence.

3. Accepting the First Estimate Without Professional Review

Insurance companies bank on homeowners accepting first estimates without question. Never accept an estimate without having your roofer review it. Discrepancies justify supplements or formal disputes.

4. Hiring an Unlicensed Contractor

Unlicensed roofers often submit poor estimates, handle insurance coordination badly, and perform substandard work. Work with licensed, insured contractors. It protects you, your home, and your insurance claim.

5. Signing an Assignment of Benefits (AOB) Without Understanding It

Some contractors ask you to assign insurance benefits to them directly. This means they collect insurance payments instead of you. This can:

  • Limit your negotiating power
  • Create contractual obligations you don't control
  • Complicate disputes or supplements

Understand AOBs fully before signing. Many insurance companies now refuse to work with AOBs. Consult an attorney if unsure.

6. Not Filing Supplemental Claims

Homeowners leave $5,000-$15,000 on the table by not pursuing supplements for hidden damage. Always ask your contractor to file supplements for damage discovered during repair.
